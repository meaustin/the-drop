# The Drop — Go-to-Market Plan

### Local Pilot Phase

*Working title. Status: Initial draft — v0.2. Date: June 11, 2026. Companion to the MVP Spec.*

-----

## 1. Objective of This Phase

The goal of this phase is **not revenue.** It is to:

1. Validate the problem — confirm venue owners actually care about the thing this solves (dwell time, slow periods, repeat visits).
1. Recruit **2–3 design partners** to launch with and learn from.
1. Produce a **live reference venue** — one room where the experience visibly works, which becomes the sales tool for every venue after it.

Success in this phase is measured by *patrons actually playing and the room lighting up* — not by how many owners said yes.

-----

## 2. Strategy at a Glance

- **Local-first, founder-led.** Start with places you already frequent. Being a regular is the warmest possible intro and the scarcest resource at this stage — trust.
- **Learning, not selling.** Walk in to recruit a partner and understand their world, not to close. Selling gets polite nods; “help me shape this” gets the truth.
- **In-person over email.** Owners are slammed and treat cold email as spam. Email’s job is the *follow-up*, not the first touch.
- **Density over spread.** A cluster of venues in one area beats one each in many — you can show up in person, word travels between local owners, and patrons start recognizing the brand across venues.
- **Make the first “yes” risk-free.** Free, you do all the setup, instantly reversible.
- **Land and expand.** The hard part is getting in. Once you’re in with a happy room, every later feature is a low-cost upsell.

-----

## 3. Ideal First-Venue Profile

The best early pilots tend to share these traits. Use it to prioritize, not to disqualify.

- **Motivated slow periods** — dead weeknights or afternoons they actively want to fill.
- **A lingering, social crowd** — neighborhood bar, coffee shop, taproom — not a transactional or too-loud-to-engage room.
- **Owner-operated, single location** — one decision-maker, no corporate approval chain.
- **An existing community/regulars vibe** for the comradery hook to amplify.
- **Bonus: already runs trivia or events** — proves they value engagement programming.
- **Bonus: already has a screen** — makes the house screen effortless (nice-to-have, never required).

-----

## 4. Target Venues

### Primary — Mo’s Place (Playa del Rey)

*203 Culver Blvd. ~25-year neighborhood sports bar / dive. Owner-operated by Mo, who is regularly on-site greeting guests.*

**Why it’s a strong first pilot:**

- Owner is present and accessible — the shortest possible path to a yes.
- Already runs a **Trivia Night** plus DJ and live music — they already believe this kind of thing works here.
- **12+ screens** for sports — the house screen costs them nothing to enable.
- A real regulars/neighborhood community — exactly the crowd the comradery hook is built for.
- Sports-bar audience means natural sports-trivia content and crowds that linger over games.

**Tailored angle:** “You already prove trivia works here on your trivia night — this is the always-on version that runs itself on the nights you *don’t* have a host, fills your slower afternoons and weeknights, and uses the screens you already have. No host to book, no work for your staff.”

**Who/when:** Talk to Mo (or the manager) directly, in a slow weekday afternoon window — not a bartender mid-rush.

### Strong Second — Three Weavers (Inglewood)

*1031 W. Manchester Blvd. Award-winning independent craft brewery and tasting room. Mission: “It’s more than beer, it’s community.”*

**Why it fits:**

- Community is literally their stated mission — this amplifies their own brand, not just adds a gimmick.
- Multiple big-screen TVs (house screen is easy), board games available, spacious seating — they already invest in in-room engagement and lingering.
- Clear slow periods — weekday hours open at 3pm — that the ambient game is well suited to fill.
- Food-truck nights are natural high-traffic moments to debut a drop.
- Craft-beer trivia and “guess the beer” content fit perfectly, and a brewery is a prime future candidate for sponsored questions about its own beers.

**The wrinkle:** It’s a larger, established operation, so the decision-maker is likely a **taproom or events manager** rather than a single owner — expect a slightly longer path and possibly a second conversation up the chain.

**Tailored angle:** “This is built for exactly your kind of room — people who already linger over good beer and board games. It fills the quieter weekday taproom hours and deepens the community thing that’s already your whole identity.”

**Who/when:** Ask staff who runs events/the taproom; the tasting room line and an events contact are the way in. Weekday afternoon, before the evening fills.

## 5. The Outreach Motion

1. **Lead in person, warm.** Start at the places where you’re a known face. Open as a regular who loves the spot and is building something.
1. **Get to the decision-maker.** Owner or manager only — bartenders can’t say yes, and a great bartender chat that never reaches the owner is wasted.
1. **Pick the slow window.** Mid-afternoon on a weekday. Never during a rush.
1. **Show, don’t tell.** Run a clickable demo on your phone right there so they *feel* a drop. (Build this before the first meeting — it’s your most important sales asset.)
1. **Ask, then listen.** Use the question guide (Section 8) to learn more than you pitch.
1. **Make the low-risk offer** (Section 9) and aim to leave with either a pilot date or a clear next step.
1. **Follow up by email** with the one-pager and a recap (Section 7).
1. **Once one is live, it becomes the pitch.** Bring the next prospect by, or show video of the room reacting. Each success compounds into the next conversation.

-----

## 6. The In-Person Pitch (≈45 seconds)

> “Hey [Mo / name] — you know I’m in here all the time. I’m building something I think would be a great fit for this place, and I wanted your honest take before anything else.
> 
> The short version: it’s a game that runs quietly in the background while people are here. Every so often a trivia question pops up on everyone’s phone at once — a quick race — and the fastest right answer wins a little something, like a free [drink/treat]. There’s a leaderboard, so regulars come back to climb it. It basically turns a normal night into a fun little shared moment, and gives people a reason to stay for one more.
> 
> [For Mo’s:] You already do trivia night, so you know this kind of thing works here — this is the always-on version that runs itself the rest of the week, on the screens you’ve already got.
> 
> I’m not trying to sell you anything. I’m looking for one or two places to launch with, and I’d set the whole thing up for free, run it for a few weeks, and we’d kill it instantly if it ever annoyed your customers. Can I show you what a question looks like?”

*Then pull out the demo. Then move into the questions below.*

## 8. First-Conversation Question Guide

The aim is to learn about their *actual* world, not to get a polite “sure, I’d try it.” Ask about what they already do and what’s already happened — that’s where the truth is.

**Good questions:**

- “What are your slowest nights or times of day? What have you tried to fill them?”
- *(Mo’s)* “What made you start trivia night? What’s worked about it, and what hasn’t?”
- “When a DJ or an event does well here, what does ‘working’ actually look like to you — more covers, people staying longer, coming back?”
- “Walk me through a typical [slow night]. What are people doing?”
- “Have you ever tried any app, loyalty, or tech thing to get people to come back? What happened with it?”
- “What would make something like this clearly *not* worth it for you?”

**Avoid:**

- “Would you use a trivia app?” — hypothetical; invites a polite yes that teaches you nothing.
- Anything that asks them to predict their future behavior. Ask what they *do*, not what they *would* do.

-----

## 9. The Pilot Offer

Make the first yes nearly free of risk:

- **Free** for the pilot period.
- **You do all the setup** — QR table tents, the content, the house-screen URL, the prize configuration.
- **Bounded** — a defined run of ~3–4 weeks.
- **Instantly reversible** — “if it ever annoys your customers, we kill it that second.”
- **Agreed signals up front** — at this stage mostly qualitative: did people play, did the room light up, does the owner feel it was a net positive. Keep it simple and honest.

-----

## 10. Launch-Night Activation

Venue acquisition is only half the battle — a live venue with no players on night one will make the owner churn fast. Treat the first night as its own mini-product:

- **Table tents / coasters** with a clear “Scan to play — a question could drop any minute.”
- **A staff prompt** — have a bartender mention it: “We’ve got a trivia thing going, scan the code, free [treat] if you’re fastest.”
- **Seed the habit** — schedule a guaranteed prize drop early in the night so the first players get an immediate win and the room sees it happen.
- **Be there in person** for the first few nights to watch reactions, fix friction live, and capture video for the next pitch.

-----

## 11. After the First Wins

- **Owner-to-owner referral is the real engine.** Owners know owners; a happy pilot is your best introduction to the next place. Lean on this before anything else.
- **Saturate the area / a venue type.** Build density in Playa del Rey / the Westside, or focus on one type (neighborhood bars, or taprooms) so your content and pitch stay sharp.
- **Secondary channels, later:** local business groups, the Inglewood / Westside chambers of commerce, local owner networks — useful once you have proof, not before.

-----

## 12. Watch-Outs

- **The goodwill-yes trap.** Because these are your spots, some owners will say yes to be kind, then never push it. Make sure at least some pilots have real commercial motivation, not just rapport — and judge by whether patrons played, not by the yes.
- **Don’t over-build before the first conversation.** A clickable demo beats a finished app for learning. Talk to Mo and Three Weavers before polishing anything.

-----

## 13. Open Questions / Next Steps

- How many pilots to run at once (2–3 is the likely sweet spot for learning without spreading thin).
- The exact pilot success bar — what would make you confident enough to keep building vs. rethink.
- When/whether to introduce pricing after a successful pilot, and what shape it takes.
- The clickable demo — the single most important thing to build before the first meeting.
